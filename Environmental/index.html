Environment

“The planned demolition of the Aylesbury & Heygate estates is the social, financial and environmental crime of the decade by a local authority.”

Donnachadh McCarthyFormer Southwark Councillor & Environmentalist

We object not just to the social and financial cost of demolishing the Heygate estate, but also to the environmental cost.

Carbon Emissions: The carbon debt incurred by the original demolition and rebuild of the Heygate area 35 years ago, will remain in the atmosphere for a further 65 years. The huge carbon debt proposed for yet another comprehensive demolition and rebuild will remain in the atmosphere for another 100 years. This seriously compromises both the Government’s CO2 reduction targets and Bill Clinton’s praise for the scheme as ‘carbon positive sustainable growth’.

The current masterplan claims to be undertaking the entire regeneration with a zero increase in carbon emissions. However, what it fails to point out is that its calculations are based purely on ‘operative’ carbon emission forecasts. What it fails to include are the ‘embedded’ carbon emissions, i.e. all the carbon emissions resulting not just from the construction of the new buildings, but also those from the original construction of the Heygate estate.

The buildings on the Heygate estate were built to last at least 60 years, and according to the most recent survey they are set to last many decades beyond this. They are constructed with 8”-thick reinforced high-grade concrete panels. Concrete is one of the best insulating materials that can be used in creating energy efficient homes.

We believe that the full-scale demolition of the Heygate estate is not the most sustainable option, and this is backed by extensive academic research1. We plan to innovatively and sustainably refurbish a number of the Heygate’s buildings, based around the recommendations made in this 1998 council-commissioned appraisal study

Trees and Green Space: The Heygate estate currently boasts 1.93 hectares of lush green amenity space, containing an urban forest of around 450 mature trees. The current masterplan will see the destruction of the vast majority of these trees and amenity spaces. We are discussing alternative plans which would involve retaining all of the trees and opening up the amenity spaces with cycle routes and pathways.

Transport: 5,000 children are killed or injured every year as a result of road traffic accidents. Our plan will separate pedestrians from traffic by creating a system of walkways across the E&C area, similar to those that can be found at the Barbican Centre or on the Heygate estate. This will significantly reduce the risk of road traffic accidents whilst allowing traffic to flow more freely.


Northern Roundabout: The E&C northern roundabout is officially the most dangerous accident blackspot for cyclists in London. The current masterplan fails to address this and actively encourages cars over public transport: it plans to build parking spaces for 25% of the new homes, despite the fact that local planning policy only allows for 10%. The Better Elephant regeneration plans will restrict parking to a maximum 5% of new homes, and transform the northern roundabout into a brand new ‘Civic Square’ pedestrian zone.

Shopping Centre: The Elephant & Castle shopping centre currently stands in a key position between the northern roundabout and the rail station. Misguided utopian planning of the 1960s left its legacy in this asbestos-ridden eyesore, which is beginning to show signs of structural deficiencies. Better Elephant plans will see this monolithic giant removed to provide easy access from the civic square to the mainline, bakerloo and northern line stations.

Civic Square: The Civic Square will be similar in size to Trafalgar Square, and will provide a venue for festivals, exhibitions and screenings. Trees will be planted, and stylish street furniture, pavillions, public art and water features will be installed.







MUSCo Our vision for regeneration is also mindful on its potential impact on the environment. We are planning to create a MUSCo (Multi-utilities Services Company), which will supply the entire E&C area with heat, power, non-potable ‘green’ water and data services. The sustainable community energy system will connect individual sites to a community heating network, compatible with locally derived sources of renewable fuel. It will be capable of delivering heat and power to 6.5m square feet of development and of being extended to serve the wider community.
