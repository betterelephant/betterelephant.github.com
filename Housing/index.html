“There are failed housing estates, but this is not one of them.”

Tim Tinker, original Heygate estate architectGuardian Article Feb 2011

Page 82 of a council-commissioned survey of the Heygate estate states that: ‘Many of the residents on the Heygate estate have lived on the estate since its construction 25 years ago, and many have lived in the locality all their lives.’

It goes on to make recommendations against full demolition because ‘The social effect of the above would be to completely break up the Heygate community’.

Southwark council chose to ignore the recommendations of its own report, and began ‘decanting’ the estate in 2007. By the end of 2008, most of the estate’s residents had been disbanded throughout the borough by forcing them through the bidding procedure for existing council stock. The Heygate’s residents were given a ‘right to return’ to the Elephant & Castle- but with a number of restrictions:

  1. The right to return does not apply to the developments planned for the Heygate footprint itself. It only pertains to the so-called ‘early housing sites’- Only three of which have been completed to date.

  2. Validity of only 7 years…

Needless to say that only 147 residents have signed up to it..

This forced displacement of a community is a clear violation of human rights law:“forcible transfer of population” is defined as a crime against humanity by the Rome Statute of the International Criminal Court (Article 7).

And a violation of the 1976 Race Relations Act. Indeed, the E&C regeneration scheme has already been referred to the Commission for Racial Equality.

Despite having been declared structurally sound in a survey commissioned by the council, the current regeneration scheme plans to demolish the 1,212 spacious but neglected council homes on the Heygate estate, and replace them with 2,500 new ‘city appartments’. Only 12.5% of the new homes planned to be built are designated for social-rented accommodation, and it is still not clear if these will classed under the new ‘affordable rent’ category (80% of market rent).

This is significantly below the requirements set out by Southwark council’s own planning policy, and the London Plan framework for new developments in the capital. This is a net loss of nearly 1,000 social rented homes at a time when they are most needed (19,000 families currently on Southwark’s housing list). It will serve only to exacerbate the hardship resulting from the current economic crisis, and will result in the existing community being priced out of their neighbourhood.

In addition only 10% of the new homes built are due to be family homes (3-bedroom). The rest are set to be small 1 or 2 bedroom studio flats, which - if the Strata Tower is anything to go by - will be bought up by investment companies on a buy-to-let basis.

We don’t believe that this will create a ‘sustainable community’, just a transitory population of tenants with little commitment to the area or their neighbours. The London Plan requires that a minimum of 35% of all new housing built in London is social-rented accommodation. It also requires 40% of these to be 4 bedroom units or larger.

The current plans are therefore in breach of the requirements set out by the London-wide planning policy. Southwark council has attempted to circumvent this by producing a get-out clause in its local planning policy [E&C SPD]. However, this document is breach of national planning policy because it failed to consult….

Better Elephant plans to retain and refurbish all of the family-size housing units on the Heygate estate by purchasing them under the ‘Community Right to Buy’ introduced by the new Localism Act.

There have already been a number of imaginative proposals for refurbishing the estate, and altering buildings for alternative use (ie. converting garages to artist studios/workshops etc.). Similar schemes in other locations - like the Park Hill estate in Sheffield have proven to be a resounding success.



Examples are all around of the miserable, yet grinning regeneration tat that awaits. Across the road from the Heygate is a nasty block clad in Trespa, a thin, tinny material used to make concrete frames enclosing tiny flats look bright and shiny; nearby is Strata, a flashy tower with three non-functional wind turbines at the top, aimed squarely at the luxury market. As against the Heygate’s unfashionable simplicity, it’s a building that takes the Bruce Grobbelaar approach to design: making the easy – a tall tower of apartments – look fiendishly difficult; a cluttered and clumsy design that was the deserved winner of Building Design’s Carbuncle Cup award for the worst building constructed in 2010. The Heygate, harsh as it may seem, treats its residents as adults and serves a much-needed social function: keeping low-income families in the centre of London. The regeneration that aims to erase it is marked by the infantile, jolly aesthetic that so often accompanies acts of class-cleansing today.
